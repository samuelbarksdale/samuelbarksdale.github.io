Analyzing the achievement gap between Black and White students in Philadelphia School Districts¶

Samuel Barksdale, Fadil Ibrahim¶

Introduction¶

The purpose of this project is to analyze the achievement gap between white and black students that are juniors in High School in the School District of Philadelphia, our home district.¶

Through this tutorial we aim to determine whether there is a gap present, as well as how wide it is, using performance levels obtained from the results of the English Language Arts (ELA), Mathematics, and Science portions of the Pennsylvania System of School Assessment (PSSA) exam. We use this exam as well as the Algebra I, Biology, and Literature exams specifically for 11th graders to assess the disparity in academic performance.¶

Data collection:¶

We'll first start by collecting data from the school district's website from the District Performance Office (https://www.philasd.org/performance/programsservices/open-data/pssakeystone/)

To get a broad range of data, we go back 5 years starting at the 2013-2014 school year results. The results for each year contain plenty of data including schools, categories of classification, the number of students tested, as well as how many scored either:

  1. Below Basic
  2. Basic
  3. Proficient
  4. Advanced
  5. Proficient + Advanced

...and the percentages for the corresponding counts.

Data Processing:¶

The next step in the procedure is to process the data we have obtained and make it more organized and tidy. We do this, so that the sets of data for each year are uniform and easy to work with when it comes time to analyze it.

Because we are solely looking for data connecting race with performance as it pertains to the entire school district, we get rid of certain columns including school name, school id number or code, the counts of each performance level (leaving just the percentage columns), and what learning network the students came from.

Initial Findings¶

Hmm, It does appear that a much higher percent of African American students received scores of Below Basic than White students on the Biology portion. Approximately ~23% gap exists in this area.

The Algebra I test also shows a similar trend. African American Students are significantly higher than White students. The gap is not as large however. The gap that exists here is only approximately 17%.

The next thing to look at is how Black students compare to White students in the placement of Advanced test scores.

Looking at the Biology scores over time for African American students, there does appear to be a positive increase trend of the percentages of Black Students placing in Advanced. This is very good to see, however the gain is not by much though.

The trend in Algebra I is not as noticeable however. There does appear to be a very slight increase, but not too significant.
